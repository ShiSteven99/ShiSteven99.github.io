Engineering

Technical Work

On the application engineering side, the work was focused on robot programming and ensuring reliable communication between the robot and any other equipment — PLCs, vision systems, sensors, and end-of-arm tooling (EOAT). The core idea was finding the right application for each customer to implement robotics in a way that made sense for their business. The key applications I led are vision-guided bin picking, palletizing, screwdriving, machine tending, quality inspection, and welding/plasma cutting.

For welding, the bottleneck was almost always labor. It's genuinely hard to find qualified welders, so companies end up turning down jobs they can't staff and get stuck in place. For machine tending, operators were spending most of their shift just loading and unloading CNC machines or presses — time that could go toward something that actually requires a person, especially since most of these parts were repetitive. For palletizing, it was physical strain and end-of-shift fatigue. It's also one of the hardest roles to staff consistently given how demanding the work is.

For vision-guided bin picking, this has become very popular in recent years as 3D cameras have been equipped with more powerful AI models. Previously, you had to have someone sort through a random bin of parts or stage each part in a repeatable position for the robot to work. Newer technologies have made it possible to eliminate that intermediate step in certain cases, though it's still a challenging application — especially with parts that tangle, are deformable, or are highly reflective. For quality inspection, it came down to repeatability; people get tired and miss things, and a defect that slips through has costly downstream consequences. Screwdriving was similar — torque consistency is hard to maintain manually over thousands of cycles, making it one of those extremely repetitive tasks that cobots are well-suited for, depending on the torque requirements.

The engineering challenge across all of it was the same: figure out exactly what the robot needs to sense and respond to, then set up the right communication between everything — the PLC, the end-of-arm tooling, any vision system, and the robot controller itself.

Partnerships & Growth

Building the Ecosystem

Distributors, Integrators & OEMs
Making Sure Partners Are Well Equipped

After about a year, I started leading the applications side as well as stepping into business development. I focused on expanding our market reach by building and strengthening relationships across the automation ecosystem, including distributors, system integrators, OEM partners, and complementary brands.

To ensure a successful partnership, no matter the type, one of the most important things is providing them with the necessary tools to be successful with our robot. So a big part of my role was building out a training curriculum consisting of an online video library as well as structuring in person hands on training. For distributors, it was also about making sure they had the right marketing materials and support to actually sell the robot, and for integrators and OEMs it was more about the technical training to make sure they could successfully implement the robot in their projects.

With my background in machine learning and the rapid rise of AI tools, I saw a clear opportunity to improve how we approached outreach. I put together a small team of interns and started experimenting with different tools to find practical ways to make both our marketing and internal processes more efficient. We used platforms like Make and n8n to automate tasks such as lead generation and follow-ups, and leveraged Claude and Gemini for lead research and content creation. As we iterated, this gradually shifted our approach away from manual, one-off efforts toward something much more streamlined and scalable. Along the way, we also built out a few internal tools for things like inventory tracking and document generation, which helped improve efficiency on the operations side as well. Overall, it became less about using AI for its own sake and more about building simple, reliable systems that saved time and allowed us to focus on higher-value work.

Looking Back

Biggest Takeaway

Customers who had a bad first automation experience were really hard to re-engage — it didn't matter how good the next proposal was, they were burned. So I started putting a lot of energy into making sure the first project was actually set up to succeed, even if that meant passing on things that looked interesting on paper. The goal wasn't to take every project, it was to make sure the ones we took actually went well.
